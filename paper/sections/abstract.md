# Abstract

Optical Character Recognition (OCR) for African languages remains underdeveloped, particularly for tone languages such as Yorùbá, where diacritics are semantically contrastive: the unmarked base form *ogun* maps to entirely different words—war, medicine, inheritance, or the number twenty—depending solely on tonal marking. Existing systems including PaddleOCR, Tesseract, and multimodal large language models consistently fail to distinguish diacritic minimal pairs such as *ògún*, *ògùn*, and *ogún*, producing transcriptions that alter meaning. This is compounded by the absence of Yorùbá OCR benchmarks sensitive to tonal morphology.

We present three contributions. First, a curated dataset of 2,945 annotated line crops from the *Yorùbá di Wúrà* educational series (Books 1–6), authored by co-authors of this paper, with a strict book-level train/test split to evaluate cross-font generalization. Annotations are human-corrected, UTF-8 normalized (NFC), and constrained to a 99-character dictionary encoding Standard Yorùbá's full diacritic space. The dataset and code will be publicly released. Second, we fine-tune PaddleOCR-VL-1.5 with LoRA and benchmark it against PaddleOCR PP-OCRv4, Tesseract, and Qwen 2.5 VL under zero-shot and fine-tuned settings. Third, we introduce Diacritic Error Rate (DER), a metric isolating tone-specific recognition failures from general character errors.

The LoRA fine-tuned model achieves the lowest CER (96.5%) and DER (77.6%) across all systems, reducing DER by 19.0 percentage points relative to the strongest zero-shot baseline on diacritic fidelity. Multimodal models occasionally recover the correct word meaning through contextual inference despite diacritic errors—selecting the right sense of *ogun* from surrounding context, for instance—but their tonal fidelity at the character level remains inconsistent, making them unsuitable for faithful text digitization. This work establishes the first reproducible Yorùbá OCR benchmark.
